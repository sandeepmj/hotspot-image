New Yorkers Stand With Muslims and Await New Executive Order

By Joy Notoma and Sarah Stein Kerr

On a sunny afternoon in mid-February, New Yorkers gathered to stand in solidarity with Muslims affected by the Trump administration’s executive order, which banned citizens from seven Muslim-majority countries from entering the United States.

The “I Am a Muslim, Too,” rally, held in Times Square on February 19, 2017, brought together Muslims and allies to express their anger and disapproval of the order.

Following the suspension of the travel ban by three federal judges, immigrants and Muslims now wait in suspense. A revised order is expected to be announced in the coming days.

At least 3 million people in the US have taken part in protests and rallies since Trump’s inauguration. But in addition to protests and rallies, New Yorkers have engaged in activities such as postcard writing, stress relief programs, and small political gatherings in reaction to Trump’s election.

Reporters interviewed protesters to learn more about how they are feeling, what they are thinking about and what they are doing during this moment of uncertainty.

Immigration policy has been a source of tension for US presidents since George Washington. John Casey, a Professor of Public and International Affairs at Baruch College, urged the Trump administration to not overlook the complexity of immigration to this country.

"The US has to realize that [immigration] is not just a tap you can turn on or off. I think they will find it’s more complicated than they imagined,” said Casey.
